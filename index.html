Research

Deep Learning Approach for Complex Image Processing Problems: Case Study with Explosion Detection and Satellite Image Segmentation

This thesis explored how deep learning can be applied to solve complex image processing problems without traditional feature-engineering steps. It focused on two case studies: explosion detection from CCTV footage and river path segmentation from satellite imagery. For explosion detection, both supervised and semi-supervised approaches were developed using models such as CNN, CNN-LSTM, CNN-GRU, VGG16, and GANs, achieving up to 99.9% accuracy in real-time classification tasks. A custom YOLOv5 model was also trained for fire localization, demonstrating the system’s potential in real-world safety monitoring. The semi-supervised GAN model proved capable of maintaining high accuracy even with limited labeled data, showing strong potential for scalable, data-efficient applications.

The second part of the research focused on geospatial data analysis using a pix2pix GAN model to segment river paths from high-resolution satellite images. Data were collected and annotated manually using EarthExplorer and ArcGIS, producing paired training datasets for model learning. The GAN architecture, consisting of a U-Net generator and PatchGAN discriminator, achieved effective segmentation with low loss values (L1 loss of 0.09), accurately distinguishing river bodies from surrounding land. This work demonstrates how deep learning can automate environmental mapping and disaster-prevention analysis while reducing dependence on heavy GIS software. Overall, the study contributes to both technical innovation and social impact, merging artificial intelligence with safety and environmental monitoring applications.

Key Findings & Contributions:
  • Achieved high-accuracy event detection: Developed supervised and semi-supervised deep learning models (CNN, CNN-LSTM, GAN) for explosion detection, reaching up to 99.9% classification accuracy and demonstrating reliable real-time safety monitoring.
  • Built data-efficient models: Showed that GAN-based semi-supervised learning can maintain strong performance with limited labeled data, reducing dependency on large, annotated datasets.
  • Advanced geospatial segmentation: Created a pix2pix GAN pipeline for precise river path segmentation using ArcGIS-annotated satellite imagery, achieving low L1 loss (0.09) and robust water-body delineation.
  • Bridged AI and environmental analysis: Demonstrated how deep learning can support public safety, environmental mapping, and disaster prevention, highlighting the broader social impact of AI-driven spatial analysis.

Can we perform topic modelling and extract themes from mental health conversational data?
In this piece I explore how natural language processing — in particular Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic modelling — can be applied to mental-health conversational data (therapist-patient dialogues) to uncover the major themes and insights embedded within. Using a publicly available dataset of nearly 1,000 patient statements I cleaned, transformed and modelled the text to discover dominant topics. The results show promise for building systems that summarise session content, detect topic trends across multiple sessions, and ultimately support therapists by highlighting underlying themes or warning signals.
NLP Topic Modelling Mental Health LDA
Spotify Data Analysis using R and ggplot2
In this project I used the spotifyr package in R to pull data on my top 98 most-listened tracks and then employed ggplot2 (and supporting tidyverse tools) to explore what listening patterns emerge. I visualised distributions of popularity, keys, loudness, energy, acousticness, and grouped tracks by popularity-class and key to uncover personal listening preferences. For example I found that many of my favourite tracks fall into a mid-popularity range (about 35–70) and tend to be in the key of C, and that loudness shows a roughly linear relationship with energy.

Image Noise -Basic of computer vision (Part-02)
The article explains the concept of image noise in computer vision and how it affects different types of images such as binary, greyscale, and RGB. It discusses common causes of noise including lighting variations, surface reflections, and sensor limitations, and describes how noise can be represented as a random variable following distributions like Gaussian or uniform. The piece also distinguishes between additive and multiplicative noise models (for example, Gaussian noise and salt-and-pepper noise) and lays the groundwork for later discussions on image filtering and convolution.

Understanding Image Derivative (Basic of computer vision)
The article explores the concept of image derivatives in computer vision by extending the mathematical idea of a derivative (rate of change) to image data represented as a 2D array of pixel values. It demonstrates how differences between adjacent pixel values can be computed using simple masks (such as [-1, 1]) to approximate the derivative along the x-axis, and highlights that analogous operations apply in the y-direction for 2D images. The piece serves as a foundation for understanding convolution, correlation and filtering in future discussions.
